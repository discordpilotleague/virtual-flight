## A complete ground-up rebuild

The biggest single fact about Teterboro V2 is that it is not a patch. Dreamflight Studios — the team behind Jackson Hole (KJAC) and McClellan-Palomar (KCRQ) — discarded the original asset library and rebuilt every building, every taxiway and every piece of ground clutter from scratch. According to the developer's own description on Contrail, the airport was rebuilt "using extensive real-world references and years of iteration" so that V2 captures "not only the layout, but the true scale and character that define operations at Teterboro."

The decision to start over wasn't gratuitous. The V1 release drew criticism in the community for stuttering when paired with other New York-area sceneries — KJFK, KLGA, KEWR — and for visuals that aged quickly once MSFS 2024 raised the bar for ground polygons and PBR. V2 attacks both fronts at the same time: a modern asset pipeline that fits the 2024 visual budget, and an optimization pass aimed at making the scenery sit comfortably alongside the rest of the New York metro stack.

## Seven detailed FBOs, custom static aircraft and a real ramp atmosphere

Teterboro is not the typical commercial airport. It is one of the busiest **fixed-base operator (FBO) hubs in the United States**, where Gulfstreams, Falcons, Globals, Citations and Phenoms taxi in a constant ballet between the seven major operators that work the field. Dreamflight has modeled each of these FBOs individually, with **interior detailing, lobbies and hangars**: Signature Aviation, Atlantic Aviation, Jet Aviation and the four others that share the field. This is genuinely rare in MSFS sceneries; most airports stop at exterior shells.

On the ramp, V2 ships with **custom static aircraft and business-jet traffic**, so even before you fire up live AI traffic from third-party tools, the airport looks alive. The ground equipment — fuel trucks, tugs, baggage carts, lavatory and water service vehicles — is appropriate for a business-aviation environment rather than the generic airliner GSE bundled with the sim. Combined with **handcrafted PBR materials** on every surface and **carefully tuned night lighting**, the result is a Teterboro that feels closer to the photographs that drew you to KTEB in the first place than the somewhat sterile V1.

## Modern ground polygon and accurate, current layout

If you've flown a recent payware airport in MSFS 2024 you know how much the **ground polygon** has become a make-or-break factor. V2 ships with an "advanced custom ground work" pass that includes high-fidelity pavement textures, realistic wear patterns, accurate runway and taxiway markings, and proper EMAS arrestor beds at runway ends. The taxiway network has been redrawn to reflect Teterboro's **current operational layout** — not the version that lived in the default Bing imagery five years ago — and the **landside areas** (parking lots, fences, access roads) have been built out so that approaches from the south do not show a hard cutoff between airport and surrounding suburb.

For pilots who care about procedural accuracy, this matters. Teterboro is famously hemmed in by **Class B airspace from KEWR, KLGA and KJFK**, and the noise-abatement procedures around runway 19 and the Teterboro 5 ILS depend on the geography being right. With V2, your published charts and your visual environment finally match.

## Teterboro in the real world: a quick refresher

For readers who are new to KTEB, a short reality check helps put the scenery into context. Teterboro Airport sits in Bergen County, New Jersey, **12 miles north-northwest of Midtown Manhattan**, and is owned by the **Port Authority of New York and New Jersey** with operations contracted to AFCO AvPORTS. Two asphalt runways serve the field — **01/19 (7,000 × 150 ft)** and **06/24 (6,013 × 150 ft)** — both equipped with high-intensity runway lighting. A tower has been on duty there since October 1975.

The airport has a federally codified **100,000-pound weight limit** in place since 1977, which is why you will never see a 737 or A320 at Teterboro: it is specifically a general-aviation and corporate-aviation field. Roughly **65 percent of operations are general aviation, 34 percent air taxi**, and the rest is military and a sliver of airline activity. In a typical pre-2020 year the field handled around **173,000 movements** — about **488 flights per day** — with a fleet that is **81 percent jet**, 11 percent helicopter and the rest piston. Around 121 aircraft are based on the 827-acre site, distributed across 19 hangars.

For virtual pilots, this profile dictates the scenery's whole personality. Teterboro is not a place to fly a heavy on instruments; it is the place to fly a **Citation Longitude, a Phenom 300, a Gulfstream G500 or a HondaJet** in and out of a busy ramp surrounded by the New York skyline. V2 finally gives that fantasy a visual setting that is on par with the aircraft most people fly there.

## Why business-jet enthusiasts will love this

Business-aviation simmers have been quietly under-served in recent years. The community has high-fidelity airliners — PMDG 737, Fenix A320, iniBuilds A350 — and stunning hub airports, but the **business-jet ramp experience** has rarely been the focus of major scenery developers. KTEB V2 changes that.

The combination of **modeled FBO interiors**, **custom static jets** and **business-jet traffic** means that whether you're starting cold and dark in front of Signature with a Citation or arriving from Aspen in a Gulfstream after midnight, the ramp tells the right story. **Night lighting** has been specifically tuned for the type of operations that happen at Teterboro — long bands of taxi lights leading to brightly lit FBO aprons, with each operator's logo and ramp signage where it should be. For pilots who fly **online networks like VATSIM or IVAO**, this is also a place where the controllers are usually attentive: KTEB approaches and departures regularly involve hand-offs to New York TRACON and ground coordination across multiple FBOs.

## Compatibility, performance, file size

Teterboro V2 supports both **MSFS 2020 and MSFS 2024** out of the same purchase, which is increasingly the standard for major payware sceneries in 2026. The V1-to-V2 upgrade has been built explicitly with optimization in mind: Dreamflight has rewritten the asset pipeline to keep draw calls under control, and early community feedback (linked below) suggests that V2 sits noticeably better than V1 in the Manhattan stack — for example, alongside payware KJFK, KLGA and KEWR.

If you fly with **PMDG, iniBuilds or Fenix** payware aircraft on a mid-tier rig, expect Teterboro V2 to behave like a polished modern scenery: heavier than a default field, lighter than a New York mega-hub, and with no obvious VRAM spikes when transitioning from cruise to ramp. Bring at least 12 GB of VRAM and 32 GB of system RAM if you also load surrounding photoreal sceneries.
