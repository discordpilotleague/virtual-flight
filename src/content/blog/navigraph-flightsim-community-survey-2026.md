Every April, Navigraph hands the microphone to the flight simulation community — and every April, more of us pick it up. The FlightSim Community Survey 2026 gathered responses from **over 42,000 simmers worldwide**, making it the largest edition to date. The results, published on April 21, paint the most detailed picture yet of who we are, what we fly, and where the hobby is heading.

## A community that keeps growing (and stays remarkably consistent)

The headline number — 42,000+ respondents — is impressive on its own, but the more revealing finding is how stable the core community looks year after year. Most simmers discovered the hobby between the ages of 10 and 19, a figure that has barely moved across multiple editions of the survey.

The tie to real-world aviation is just as striking. **Around 21% of respondents hold a pilot license**, and roughly **18% work somewhere inside the aviation industry** — pilots, controllers, engineers, dispatchers, cabin crew. For a lot of the people filling hangars on MSFS and X-Plane, this isn't a casual hobby tacked onto a weekend. It's an extension of a professional life that happens in the air.

Flying habits confirm the picture. Most simmers log **two to five sessions per week**, typically **two to three hours each**. IFR remains the dominant style of operation, followed by VFR. In other words: file a flight plan, follow procedures, talk to ATC — the virtual skies behave a lot like the real ones.

## Could flight simmers land a real plane?

Navigraph revisited a question that mainstream media loves to raise every time a simmer makes the news: could the average flight simmer actually put a real aircraft on the ground? The 2026 answer is more nuanced than the clickbait suggests.

Certified pilots reported higher confidence across nearly every aircraft type — unsurprisingly. Non-pilots edged ahead on only one narrow item: landing an **Airbus A320**, and only by a very small margin, probably reflecting how thoroughly the A320 family has been simulated and studied by third-party addon developers.

Confidence peaks where you'd expect: the **Cessna 172** leads the ranking for both groups. At the other end, fighter jets, helicopters, hot air balloons, and — delightfully — the paper plane sit at the bottom. Only about one in five respondents felt confident landing a paper plane, which is either a comment on paper-plane aerodynamics or on the limits of honesty in surveys.

## Why we fly: motivation gets its own chapter

New in 2026, Navigraph broke down what actually drives simmers to open the sim. Six categories emerged:

- **Immersion** — realism and authentic experiences
- **Creative** — visual enjoyment and personal expression
- **Mastery** — learning systems, improving procedures
- **Achievement** — goals, measurable progress, career mode
- **Social** — shared experiences, VATSIM, group flights
- **Chaos** — pushing limits, emergencies, unpredictability

Most pilots aren't single-motivation simmers: the categories overlap. But the framework is useful for developers and tool makers, because a simmer chasing *mastery* wants different things from a simmer chasing *chaos*. Navigation add-ons, study-level aircraft, and career platforms live in different quadrants of that map.

## The 2026 setup — hardware gets heavier

Performance matters, and 2026 hardware data shows the community voting with its wallet. **64 GB of RAM** is gaining ground, although **32 GB remains the most common** configuration. NVIDIA still dominates GPU share. The **RTX 4090 remains the most popular card**, but its share has dropped noticeably year-over-year as the **RTX 5080, 5070 Ti, and 5090** start appearing in setups.

On peripherals, the podium is unambiguous: **throttle quadrants lead**, followed by joysticks and rudder pedals. Home cockpit builds crept up from **9% to 10%** — a small percentage, but a significant signal. More and more simmers are dedicating a whole room to their setup, and even niche additions like seatbelts now show up in the hardware list.

Spending patterns are what you'd expect from a mature hobby. Most users sit in lower price ranges on hardware, with a smaller group of enthusiasts pushing the upper bounds. Software spending is more evenly distributed, with regular mid-range purchases and a long tail of premium addons.

## MSFS 2024 takes the crown

The single biggest platform story in 2026 is that **Microsoft Flight Simulator 2024 is now the most used simulator**, overtaking its 2020 predecessor. **X-Plane 12** holds a steady share of the community — neither collapsing nor surging.

More than **30% of respondents reported switching from MSFS 2020**. The vast majority of those who switched went to MSFS 2024, with smaller currents flowing to X-Plane 12 and other platforms. If 2025 was the year MSFS 2024 proved it could run, 2026 is the year it proved it could retain. For anyone building add-ons or maintaining a virtual airline, the strategic picture is now clear: MSFS 2024 is where the center of gravity has moved.

## What simmers actually fly

Aircraft preference data is where the survey gets spicy.

**In MSFS 2024**, the **Fenix Airbus A319/A320/A321 family leads at 43%**, with PMDG's **737 variants at 32%** and the **PMDG 777 at 23%**. That's a lot of tubes flying out of a lot of gates every evening. The Fenix dominance on short and medium haul, paired with PMDG on widebody, is arguably the most stable top-of-chart combo the sim has seen in years.

**In X-Plane 12**, the **ToLiss Airbus narrowbody family** accounts for **37%** of usage, followed by the **Zibo 737-800X at 30%**, with the **FlightFactor 777** gaining traction. The two ecosystems have clearly converged on the same real-world aircraft types — the Airbus narrowbody and the Boeing twin-aisle — even as the developer lineup differs.

## Highlights of the year and what's next

When asked about the best releases of the past year, a few products stood out. The **iniBuilds A350** ranked highest, followed by the **Fenix BFU** and **WinControl** hardware. Looking ahead, the community is hyped for the **Fenix A320 and A321 Neo**, **Bluebird's 757 and 767**, and upcoming **WinControl** hardware — the kind of lineup that will keep hangars busy well into 2027.

## Why this survey matters to pilots like us

Beyond the fun statistics, the 2026 survey confirms something that shapes the entire virtual aviation stack: **the community is larger, more serious, and more invested than ever**. Developers use these numbers to prioritize which aircraft to build next. Hardware makers read them before planning product lines. Tools for flight planning, charts, and networks shape their roadmaps around real usage data.

Navigraph has released the [full FlightSim Community Survey 2026 report](https://navigraph.com/blog/survey-2026) for free, along with the complete dataset for anyone who wants to dig deeper. Whether you fly a paper plane or a Level-D study jet, the 2026 edition confirms one thing: the virtual skies have never been this crowded — and that's very good news.
