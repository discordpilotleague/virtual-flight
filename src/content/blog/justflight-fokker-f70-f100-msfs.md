After more than two years of in-house development, Just Flight is putting the final polish on its **Fokker F70/F100 Professional Bundle** for Microsoft Flight Simulator 2020 and 2024. The May 2026 development update confirms that the AFCAS profile logic has been re-coded, the braking model refined and the project is now in the final bug-fixing pass before release.

![Fokker F100 Professional exterior screenshot in Microsoft Flight Simulator](../../assets/hero-justflight-fokker-f70-f100-msfs.webp)

## A long-overdue Fokker for the modern simulator

The Fokker F100 is a regional jet that quietly carried millions of passengers across Europe, Asia and Australia from the late 1980s onwards, with the shorter F70 entering service in 1995. KLM Cityhopper, Austrian Airlines, Air France Regional, American Airlines, QantasLink and Alliance Airlines all ran sizeable fleets, and the type still flies today with operators such as Alliance Airlines and Carpatair. Despite that real-world footprint, the Fokker family has never had a high-fidelity rendition in MSFS — Just Flight's bundle is the first project that aims to change that.

The bundle covers **five sub-variants**: one F70 with integral airstairs and a large cargo door, plus four F100 configurations covering integral airstairs and sliding-door layouts, small and large cargo doors, and the optional L2 door. Both airframes use Rolls-Royce Tay turbofans — the **Tay 620-15** on the F70 and the **Tay 650** on the heavier F100 — and Just Flight has recorded the engines from a real aircraft in the Netherlands, then layered the recordings into a Wwise-based sound pack that the developer claims contains over 500 individual samples.

## Custom-coded systems, not stock MSFS

What separates this project from a typical MSFS payware release is how much of the aircraft has been written from scratch. The **Flight Management System** is a fully custom-coded dual-CDU implementation with proper LNAV/VNAV, IRS alignment timing, flight plan import, and the original Fokker page hierarchy (DIR, MODE, TACT MODE, INIT, REF, F-PLN, TO/APPR, PROG). Above it sits an equally custom **Automated Flight Control and Augmentation System (AFCAS)** that drives lateral and vertical modes, including a Profile descent that respects altitude and speed constraints, plus a tuned autoland.

Underneath the avionics, the simulation reaches deep into the airframe. Each hydraulic system is modelled independently with electrically-driven pumps; the electrical bus has separate engine, APU, external and emergency sources with TRU loading; pressurization, bleed-air demand, anti-icing and the fuel-transfer logic all behave the way the aircraft really does. The famous tail-mounted **speedbrake and lift dumpers**, the gust lock, the alternate stab and flap controls, and the pressure-dependent gear timing are all reproduced individually rather than papered over with default MSFS animations.

## What it changes for virtual pilots

For pilots who already fly tube-liner add-ons such as the FlyByWire A320, the iniBuilds A310 or the PMDG 737, the F70/F100 will feel like a step into a slightly older school of cockpit design — analog standby instruments, curved CRT displays, classic switchgear — but with the kind of system depth those modern add-ons made the genre famous for. Captain, first officer and observer positions are all modelled with animated switches, knobs and circuit breakers; the developer has also added hidden clickspots for TOGA thrust and STD altimeter settings to keep usability reasonable in flight. For pilots looking to expand their hangar with similar study-level airliners, our [guide to the best payware aircraft for flight simulation in 2026](/blog/best-payware-aircraft-addons-2026) covers the current landscape across MSFS, X-Plane and DCS.

The Tay engines have a personality of their own. They are responsive at low altitude but climb slowly when the F100 is heavy: the development blog cites roughly 30 minutes and 156 nautical miles to reach FL350 at MTOW in ISA conditions. That is exactly the kind of constraint that turns short European hops — Amsterdam to Munich, London to Faro, Vienna to Bucharest — into proper procedural flights rather than fast-forward exercises. With a maximum range of about 1,500 nautical miles, the bundle is built for the regional networks where the real Fokker thrived.

## Cabin, EFB and the small details

The full passenger cabin is accessible and interactive. Cabin lighting, doors, interphones and galley equipment can be operated; cabin announcements, a digital music player and an Auto Cabin Crew logic are bundled in; and the LODs are tuned so that walking through the aircraft does not collapse frame rate. Each livery ships with its own custom cabin textures, and the **30 included paint schemes** — 11 for the F70 and 19 for the F100, ranging from KLM Cityhopper and Austrian Airlines to American Airlines, TAM and the Slovak Government Flight Service — cover most of the airframes a virtual pilot would actually want to fly.

The Electronic Flight Bag covers the operational side: cold-and-dark or turnaround states, fuel and payload manager with per-tank control, boarding and refuelling at realistic rates, GPU and chocks, take-off and landing performance calculators, **SimBrief OFP import**, METAR viewing, real-time map tracking and **Navigraph chart support** through a separate subscription. Compatibility with GSX, MSFS 2024's walkaround mode and the native MSFS interactive checklist system — with nearly 300 checklist items, more than 250 of them before takeoff — round out the package.

## Getting the most out of it

A good way to settle into the type is to start with a **short revenue route in Europe** — KLM Cityhopper Amsterdam to London City, or Austrian Vienna to Frankfurt — at moderate weight, so the aircraft has the climb margin to reach a reasonable cruise altitude. Once the FMS pages, the AFCAS modes and the speedbrake/lift-dumper logic feel familiar, longer legs into the Australian network with Alliance Airlines or QantasLink livery will stretch the autopilot's Profile mode and really show what the autoland does. Just Flight has also confirmed compatibility with the MSFS interactive checklist, so pilots who like to use the in-sim checklist UI will get the full nearly-300-item flow without third-party add-ons.

The official [Just Flight in-development page](https://www.justflight.com/in-development/f70-f100-professional-bundle) is the best place to track the next development update — the May 2026 entry mentioned final flight testing and bug fixing, which usually points to a release window measured in weeks rather than months.

## Conclusion

If the project lives up to what the development updates describe, the Fokker F70/F100 Professional Bundle could become the reference twin-jet regional in MSFS — the kind of aircraft that fills the gap between turboprops and the bigger A320/737 simulations.
