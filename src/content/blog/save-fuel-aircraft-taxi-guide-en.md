A Boeing 747 burns roughly **one tonne of jet fuel** during 15 minutes of taxiing. An A320, between 100 and 150 kg. Globally, the taxi phase accounts for nearly **1% of aviation's total fuel consumption** — a seemingly modest figure, but one that translates into millions of tonnes of CO2 and billions of dollars spent every year simply moving aircraft between gates and runways. The aviation industry has developed concrete solutions to reduce this waste, and serious virtual pilots on [PilotLeague](https://pilotleague.com/en/features/) know it well: ground fuel management is a competence indicator the platform measures flight after flight.

![KLM Boeing 737 towed by a TaxiBot at Schiphol Airport](/images/taxi-fuel-saving/taxibot-klm.webp)

## Why taxiing burns so much fuel

Aircraft jet engines are designed to operate at high altitude and high thrust settings. On the ground, they run at idle — the least efficient configuration possible. A CFM56 engine at ground idle burns approximately **5 to 7 kg of fuel per minute**, and a twin-engine aircraft runs two of them. For a four-engine widebody, the bill quadruples. Add waiting times on taxiways, queues before the runway, stop-and-go sequences imposed by air traffic control, and the waste accumulates quickly.

The problem is amplified at major international hubs. At Heathrow, Atlanta, or Frankfurt, a typical taxi takes 15 to 30 minutes — sometimes longer during peak hours. Every minute of engine idle represents fuel burned without producing any useful movement when the aircraft is stationary.

![KLM aircraft parked at Schiphol Airport gates — ground waiting time](/images/taxi-fuel-saving/schiphol-gates.webp)

## Technique #1: Single-Engine Taxi (SET)

The most widespread and simplest technique to implement. On a twin-engine aircraft, the principle is straightforward: shut down one engine during taxi and use only the other.

### How it works

Instead of starting both engines at the gate, the crew starts only one and begins taxiing. The second engine is started during taxi, early enough before the runway for it to reach its operating temperature and for all hydraulic systems to be powered before takeoff. The same logic applies on arrival: after vacating the runway, the crew shuts down one engine for the taxi to the gate.

### The savings

Single-engine taxi reduces taxi fuel consumption by **20 to 40%** depending on aircraft type and conditions. For an airline operating hundreds of daily flights, this translates into annual savings of several million liters of jet fuel.

### The limitations

Single-engine taxi is not always possible. Tight turns toward the shut-down engine side can be difficult (asymmetric thrust complicates the maneuver). Degraded weather conditions (icing, heavy rain) may require both engines to maintain adequate directional control. Some airlines prohibit SET on sloped taxiways or in specific airport configurations.

## Technique #2: TaxiBot — pilot-controlled towing from the cockpit

The TaxiBot, developed by **Israel Aerospace Industries**' Lahav division, represents a major advancement. It is a semi-robotic hybrid-electric vehicle that tows the aircraft from the terminal to the runway with **engines completely shut down**.

### The concept

Unlike a conventional pushback, the TaxiBot is **controlled directly by the pilot from the cockpit**. The vehicle reads the nose gear angle and follows the direction set by the pilot. Braking is handled by the aircraft's normal brakes — the TaxiBot detects the deceleration and adapts. The pilot therefore retains full control of trajectory and speed, exactly as during a normal taxi.

### The savings

The numbers are impressive: the TaxiBot consumes **95% less fuel** than the aircraft's engines during taxi. **Airbus** reports ground fuel savings of **50 to 85%** depending on taxi distance. The system exists in narrowbody (compatible with A320, B737) and widebody (compatible with A380, B747) versions.

### Deployment

The TaxiBot was certified in 2014 and completed its first commercial flight with **Lufthansa** at Frankfurt (flight LH140). In 2024, **KLM** completed the first passenger flight with TaxiBot at **Schiphol**. Airbus is actively pursuing tests as part of the HERON project.

![Aircraft landing gear on taxiway — TaxiBot connects to the nose gear](/images/taxi-fuel-saving/airbus-taxibot.webp)

## Technique #3: Electric Green Taxiing

Rather than towing the aircraft, why not install electric motors directly in the wheels?

### The systems

**Safran** and **Honeywell** developed the **EGTS** (Electric Green Taxiing System), which integrates 50 kW electric motors into the main landing gear wheels. Power comes from the aircraft's APU (Auxiliary Power Unit). The aircraft can then taxi, maneuver, and even reverse without starting its jet engines and without a pushback tractor.

**Green Taxi Solutions** is developing a similar system, promising estimated annual savings of **$306,000 per aircraft**.

### The overall savings

Electric taxiing reduces fuel consumption by approximately **4% across the entire flight cycle** — a figure that seems modest but, multiplied by thousands of daily flights, represents substantial savings. The additional benefit is the complete absence of jet thrust on the ground, reducing noise pollution and blast risk for ground personnel.

## Technique #4: Gate-holding and ATC optimization

The most immediate solutions do not involve onboard technology but rather ground traffic management.

### Gate-holding

The principle is simple: rather than pushing back the aircraft and having it wait with engines running in a queue on the taxiway, the airline keeps the aircraft at the gate until a direct taxi slot becomes available. The aircraft remains connected to the terminal's ground power, its engines off. When ATC gives the green light, taxi begins immediately with no intermediate waiting time.

### Delayed engine start

Pilots delay engine start as late as possible before leaving the gate. Every minute gained with engines off is fuel saved. This technique is particularly effective at congested airports where pushback wait times are frequent.

### Route optimization

Air traffic control can contribute by minimizing stops, optimizing taxi routes (avoiding detours), and coordinating departure sequences to reduce engine idle time. A-SMGCS (Advanced Surface Movement Guidance and Control Systems) help smooth ground traffic flow.

## Applying these techniques in MSFS

Serious virtual pilots can replicate these practices in Microsoft Flight Simulator 2024 for increased realism:

- **Single-engine taxi** — On study-level add-ons (PMDG 737, Fenix A320), you can start only one engine for taxi and spin up the second during the trip to the runway
- **Delayed engine start** — Wait until you are ready to taxi before starting the engines, rather than firing them up as soon as the aircraft is powered on
- **Gate-holding** — Complete your flight preparation (FMS, briefing, ATIS) with engines off at the gate before requesting pushback

On [PilotLeague](https://pilotleague.com/en/features/), the **Fuel Efficiency** pillar of your score measures precisely your consumption in kg/nm by flight phase — including taxi. A well-managed taxi with controlled fuel burn contributes directly to a better overall score. The post-flight analysis breaks down your consumption phase by phase with grades from A to D, allowing you to identify exactly where fuel was wasted.

## What this means

Taxi fuel savings are not a trivial detail — they are a concrete lever the industry is already pulling. Single-engine taxi has become a standard procedure at most major airlines. The TaxiBot is moving from experimental stage to commercial operations. Electric taxiing is progressing. And every technique, from the simplest gate-holding to the most technological EGTS, contributes to reducing the carbon footprint of an industry under growing environmental pressure.

For virtual pilots, integrating these practices into their flight routine adds a layer of operational realism that few simmers exploit.
