Microsoft Flight Simulator 2024 is about to receive its most substantial update yet. Sim Update 5, bundled with Aircraft and Avionics Update 4, is targeted for April and delivers five European-heritage aircraft converted to native MSFS 2024 standard, a brand-new Avidyne avionics suite, and long-awaited PlayStation VR2 support. For virtual pilots who have been tracking the transition from legacy MSFS 2020 aircraft, this is the milestone where Asobo's conversion pipeline shifts from promise to proof.

Here is a complete breakdown of what is coming and why it matters.

## Five Aircraft Converted to Native

The headline feature of AAU4 is the conversion of five aircraft — all with deep European aviation roots — from legacy MSFS 2020 compatibility mode to fully native MSFS 2024 status. Native conversion means each aircraft benefits from the new CFD-based aerodynamic model, full career mode integration, updated systems, and modern rendering. These are not cosmetic refreshes; they are ground-up reworks.

### ATR 42/72-600

The ATR turboprop family receives the most comprehensive overhaul in this batch. Both the ATR 42-600 and ATR 72-600 now run on the CFD aerodynamic model, giving them more realistic prop wash effects, asymmetric thrust behavior, and crosswind handling. Career mode integration is deep: pilots can fly passenger routes, cargo operations, and VIP charter missions. Lido navigation charts are available in the cockpit, and TCAS with Resolution Advisory capability adds a genuine layer of situational awareness during busy approach sequences.

On the livery side, Asobo has added Air Tahiti, Logan Air, and IndiGo — a selection that reflects the ATR's real-world footprint from the Pacific Islands to the Scottish Highlands to Indian regional routes. For pilots who enjoy short-field turboprop operations, the ATR pair will be among the most versatile aircraft in the hangar.

### Antonov An-2

The legendary Soviet biplane gets far more than a visual polish. The An-2 now features a full fuel and electrical system overhaul, bringing its systems modeling in line with native MSFS 2024 standards. An Electronic Flight Bag has been added — a modern concession for an aircraft that predates transistor radios — along with improved night lighting and functional windshield wipers.

The career integration is where the An-2 truly shines. Five distinct career modes are available: passenger transport, cargo hauling, crop dusting, firefighting, and skydiving operations. Each mode offers unique mission structures that take advantage of the An-2's legendary slow-speed handling and short-field capability. It is the kind of aircraft that rewards patience and stick-and-rudder skill, and the expanded career paths give pilots genuine reasons to keep flying it.

### Fokker F.VII

The Fokker F.VII, one of the most important transport aircraft of the 1920s and 1930s, receives career support for passenger, private, and cargo operations. A modular conversion system allows pilots to switch between different F.VII configurations, reflecting the historical versatility of an aircraft that served airlines, explorers, and militaries across three continents. Period-appropriate missions add historical flavor to one of the most characterful aircraft in the simulator.

### Latecoere 631

The massive French flying boat has been the subject of persistent bug reports since its MSFS 2020 introduction. AAU4 addresses more than 40 documented issues, ranging from flight model inconsistencies to graphical glitches. Visible passengers now populate the luxurious cabin interior, and new water effects improve the experience of operating from harbors and open water. The Latecoere 631 remains a niche aircraft, but these fixes transform it from a curiosity into a genuinely enjoyable way to explore coastal and island-hopping routes.

### Savoia-Marchetti S.55

The Italian twin-hull flying boat receives further refinements building on previous updates. The changes are less dramatic than those applied to the other four aircraft, but continued aerodynamic tuning and visual polish bring it closer to the quality standard Asobo is establishing for its vintage fleet.

## New Avionics: Avidyne IFD 540 and IFD 550

Beyond airframes, SU5 introduces a new avionics platform to the simulator. The Avidyne IFD 540 and IFD 550 GPS navigators arrive as drop-in replacements for the Garmin GNS 500 series, giving pilots a modern alternative with a significantly different interface philosophy.

The IFD 550 is the flagship unit and includes synthetic vision technology, rendering terrain and obstacles on the display in a way that dramatically improves situational awareness during instrument approaches and in mountainous terrain. Both the IFD 540 and IFD 550 support FAA and Lido chart display, providing real-world approach plates and airport diagrams directly on the panel.

The Avidyne units will launch initially on the Cessna 172 Classic, a deliberate choice that gives the most-flown trainer in the simulator a meaningful avionics upgrade. Expect Asobo to expand Avidyne availability to additional GA aircraft in subsequent updates. For pilots who have spent hundreds of hours staring at the GNS 530, the Avidyne interface will feel like stepping into a different cockpit — same airplane, entirely new workflow.

## PSVR2 Support

Virtual reality pilots on PC have had access to MSFS in VR since 2020, but SU5 marks the arrival of PlayStation VR2 headset support for the PC version of the simulator. The PSVR2 is a compelling VR option: it offers OLED displays with strong contrast, a wide field of view, and — critically for flight simulation — eye tracking that enables foveated rendering.

Foveated rendering concentrates GPU resources on the area where the pilot is actually looking, reducing the rendering load on peripheral vision. For a simulator as demanding as MSFS 2024, this technique can meaningfully improve frame rates without visible quality loss. Asobo confirms that more than 100 aircraft will be compatible with VR at launch, covering the majority of the fleet that virtual pilots actually fly.

For those who have been waiting for a cost-effective entry into VR flight simulation, the PSVR2 — now supported on PC via official adapter — represents a strong option compared to higher-priced headsets from Meta and Varjo.

## The Bigger Picture: 30+ Aircraft Still in Queue

SU5 and AAU4 convert five aircraft to native status, but the broader conversion effort is far from complete. More than 30 payware aircraft from MSFS 2020 remain in the upgrade queue. Aircraft and Avionics Updates 5 and 6 are already planned, suggesting a cadence of roughly one major aircraft batch per quarter through the rest of 2026.

Converting an aircraft to native MSFS 2024 standard is not a simple recompile. Each conversion involves rebuilding the aerodynamic model, integrating career missions, updating systems, and testing across the full flight envelope. For third-party developers, this also sets a benchmark: as Asobo demonstrates what a fully integrated native aircraft looks like, expectations for payware quality will inevitably rise.

## What It Means for Virtual Pilots

Sim Update 5 is not just a technical milestone; it reshapes how pilots interact with the simulator on a daily basis. The career mode expansions alone add dozens of new mission types. A pilot who previously flew the An-2 for scenic bush flights can now build an entire crop-dusting or firefighting career around it. The ATR family opens up regional airline operations that were previously the domain of third-party aircraft. And the Avidyne avionics give GA pilots a reason to revisit familiar routes with fresh eyes and new procedures to learn.

For pilots who track their flights and measure their progress, these updates create new opportunities to build a structured flying career. The diversity of mission types — from VIP charters in the ATR to skydiving drops in the An-2 — rewards versatility, and using a flight logbook or analytics tool to track each session makes the progression tangible rather than abstract.

PSVR2 support also lowers the barrier for immersive flying. Pilots who have experienced MSFS only on a flat screen will find that VR fundamentally changes spatial awareness during approaches and traffic pattern work. With flight tracking tools and performance analytics, VR pilots can quantify whether immersion translates to better flying — tighter approaches, smoother landings, more consistent altitude holds.

## Looking Ahead

April's Sim Update 5 and AAU4 represent a turning point in the MSFS 2024 lifecycle. The simulator launched with a promise that legacy aircraft would be brought up to native standard, and this update delivers meaningful proof that the pipeline works. Five aircraft spanning nearly a century of European aviation history — from the 1920s Fokker F.VII to the modern ATR 72-600 — now feel like they belong in MSFS 2024 rather than visiting from its predecessor.

With AAU5 and AAU6 on the roadmap and more than 30 aircraft still awaiting conversion, the coming months will be busy. PSVR2 support opens the door to a new segment of VR pilots. And the Avidyne avionics hint at a future where cockpit variety extends beyond airframes to the glass itself.

Whether you are a career mode enthusiast, a VR pilot exploring foveated rendering, or a systems-oriented aviator eager to learn the Avidyne workflow, SU5 has something worth your time. Track your progress and keep a detailed logbook — because every flight counts when you are measuring improvement.
